Data Center Evolution Overview

Traditional data centers were designed for CPU-focused workloads with sequential processing requirements and moderate power consumption. The emergence of AI workloads has necessitated fundamental changes to data center infrastructure, requiring significantly higher compute density, power capacity, cooling capabilities, and network bandwidth. This transformation represents a complete architectural shift from general-purpose computing to AI-optimized infrastructure.

Traditional Data Centers

Traditional data centers were designed for general-purpose computing workloads, characterized by CPU-focused architectures, sequential processing patterns, and modest power requirements. These facilities typically supported 5-10kW per rack and relied on air cooling systems.

Key Requirements

  • Compute: GPU clusters with thousands of processing cores
  • Memory: High-bandwidth memory (HBM) for fast data access

  • Storage: Parallel file systems for massive I/O
  • Power: Industrial-grade power distribution
  • Cooling: Liquid cooling systems for heat management

Power: From Outlet to Industrial

Before: Regular electrical outlet (5kW) Standard data center power
After: Industrial power (50kW+) Like powering a factory

AI workloads consume 10x more power. It's like upgrading from a regular outlet to industrial three-phase power - necessary for the massive computational requirements.

Cooling: From Fan to Swimming Pool

Before: Air conditioning Air-based cooling system
After: Liquid cooling Liquid cooling system

GPUs generate significant heat due to high power consumption. Liquid cooling systems provide superior heat removal compared to air cooling, enabling higher power density and more efficient heat management for GPU clusters.

Space: From Suburbs to Skyscrapers

Before: Horizontal sprawl Distributed layout
After: Vertical density Like skyscrapers

AI data centers pack more compute into less space - like going from suburban sprawl to urban skyscrapers, maximizing efficiency.
